Lessons Learned

1. In closely related-word games, survival matters less than information denial; if a partner is doomed, focus on making the majority word harder to reconstruct.

2. Repeated concrete markers like 'vines,' 'trellises,' 'terroir,' and 'hillside slope' can hand the eliminated wolf the exact citizen word even if the vote is correct.

3. Broad, overlap-safe language works better early than sharp contrasts; my first farming-focused answer blended somewhat, but the fresh-eating vs processing split was a noticeable probe.

4. If defending an outlier, avoid doing it in a way that creates obvious pair equity; my repeated redirects onto Frank made the Alice-Diana link easier to read.

5. When a citizen overcommits or blurts a near-neighbor word, exploit that contradiction immediately as a table-split opportunity, as with Bob saying 'winery.'

6. 'Too specific' attacks are more credible when tied to inconsistency, not just confidence; accusing Frank of steering was plausible, but the factual trees-vs-vines mismatch outweighed it.

7. For town-reading, plant-type mismatches are stronger than production-detail mismatches; citizens were right to prioritize trees vs vines over crushing/tasting-room differences.

8. Do not assume one strong domain clue fully clears someone; pair and vote-direction analysis still matters, especially when one player consistently shields another while redirecting elsewhere.

Lessons Learned

1. **Strict Information Control**: Guard the core mechanics of your word from the wolves. Avoid dropping hyper-specific identifiers too early, as giving away the exact noun gift-wraps the comeback guess for the eliminated wolf.

2. **Safe Coordinated Deception**: If a caught wolf is zeroing in on the correct guess, bluffing a highly similar decoy word is a smart counterplay. However, the bluffed word *must* seamlessly justify all citizens' prior statements. Otherwise, well-meaning teammates will panic, dispute the bluff, and confirm the true word for the wolf.

3. **Establishing Credibility**: Deploying nuanced, subtle mechanics of the deduced word (without naming it) is a powerful way to gain absolute trust from the majority early in the game.

4. **Targeting Factual Slips**: When a player describes a physical impossibility for the shared word (e.g., describing 'trees' instead of 'vines'), immediately push that factual mismatch to establish a clear voting bloc and force the wolves onto the defensive.

5. **Spotting Outlier Alliances**: Pay close attention to players who vehemently defend objectively incorrect factual statements made by others. This is often a telltale sign of a wolf attempting to shield a struggling partner.

6. **Adaptability**: Never marry your initial plan or target. If the table consensus decisively shifts or a teammate accidentally blows your cover, adapt instantly to the new narrative rather than stubbornly fighting the current.
